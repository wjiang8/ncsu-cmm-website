Research

Research areas

Our group develops and applies computational methods to predict the behavior of nuclear fuels and materials under the extreme conditions of reactor operation. Much of our work is implemented in the MOOSE framework and the BISON fuel performance code.

phase-field
Phase-Field Modeling of Fracture

Cracking governs the performance and safety margins of nearly every nuclear fuel form, from pellet-cladding interaction in UO₂ to buffer-layer fracture in TRISO particles. We develop variational phase-field fracture methods that capture crack nucleation, propagation, and branching without ad hoc criteria. Current efforts include rigorous treatment of irreversibility constraints, coupling with thermal and irradiation-driven fields, and efficient numerical solution strategies for large-scale engineering simulations.

triso / fcm
TRISO and FCM Fuel Performance

Tristructural isotropic (TRISO) coated particle fuel is the enabling technology for high-temperature gas-cooled reactors and many microreactor concepts. We model the thermomechanical response and failure probability of TRISO particles and fully ceramic microencapsulated (FCM) fuel compacts, including statistical failure analysis, cohesive zone modeling of layer debonding, and homogenization methods for particle-bearing fuel forms.

ml-surrogates
AI and Machine Learning for Fuel Performance

High-fidelity fuel simulations are computationally expensive, limiting their use in uncertainty quantification and reactor-scale analysis. We develop machine-learning surrogates and multifidelity approaches — including active learning and reduced-order models — to accelerate fuel performance prediction, in support of national initiatives such as the DOE Genesis Mission.

methods
Multiphysics and Multiscale Method Development

Underpinning our applications is a sustained effort in numerical method development: interface-enriched finite element methods, contact and cohesive zone algorithms, and coupling strategies for problems spanning microstructure to engineering scales. We contribute these capabilities to the MOOSE ecosystem.
